Comparative Genomics

1. Sharma V, Hecker N, Roscito JG, Foerster L, Langer BE, Hiller M. A genomics approach reveals insights into the importance of gene losses for mammalian adaptations. Nature Communications 2018 Mar 23;9(1):1215-1223.
Impact: Inactivation of a protein-coding gene is often associated with the loss of a function resulting in the loss of a phenotype. In this study, we developed the first computational method to screen genome alignments and detect inactivated genes in different species at a very high accuracy and with minimal manual curation. More importantly, we showed that gene losses have conferred various morphological, physiological and metabolic adaptations to different placental mammalian species. Therefore, this study shows that gene losses may not be neutral or deleterious and in fact have been evolutionary engines in different species.

2. Sharma V, Hiller M. Losses of human disease-associated genes in placental mammals. Nucleic Acids Research Genomics and Bioinformatics 2019 (doi: 10.1093/nargab/lqz012).
Impact: Genome wide association studies have identified several thousand genes in the human population where a loss-of-function mutation is associated with the development of a disease phenotype. In this study, we showed numerous examples of genes where a loss-of-function mutation is tolerated in a non-human species i.e. there is no development or progression of a disease. This study therefore highlights the existence of naturally occurring compensatory genetic mechanisms in other non-human mammalian species that prevents the onset/progression of a disease and a better understanding of these compensatory mechanisms could potentially be used to develop newer therapies for human diseases.

3. Kirilenko BM, Munegowda C, Osipova E, Jebb D, Sharma V, Blumer M, et al. Integrating gene annotation with orthology inference at scale. Science. 2023 Apr 28;380(6643):eabn3107. doi: 10.1126/science.abn3107
Impact: An accurate annotation of protein-coding genes in different mammalian species is central to all comparative sequence analyses. However, this is not a trivial task given that different genome assemblies are not of consistently good quality and gene duplication or loss events make the inference of orthology quite challenging. In this study, we developed a machine-learning based classifier that allows an accurate discrimination of orthologs from paralogs and also produces pairwise or multiple sequence alignments that are amenable to additional downstream analyses. Moreover, we annotated protein-coding genes in close to 500 mammalian genome assemblies, so this study provides a tremendous resource for further analyses of gene loss, gene gain and other molecular evolutionary analyses at genome-scale levels.

7. Sharma V and Hiller M. Loss of Enzymes in the Bile Acid Synthesis Pathway Explains Differences in Bile Composition among Mammals. Genome Biology and Evolution. 2018 Dec 1;10(12):3211-3217
Impact: This study uncovers the genomic basis underlying deviations from the typical mammalian bile acid composition by identifying repeated loss of the CYP8B1 gene in naked-mole rats, elephants, and manatees, explaining their lack of cholic acid. It further reveals that species lacking bile acids entirely have lost additional nonessential bile acid-related genes, highlighting how essential genes are retained while pleiotropic genes may be lost if compensated. These findings elucidate evolutionary mechanisms shaping bile acid metabolism diversity in mammals.
